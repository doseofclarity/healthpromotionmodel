Ottawa Charter for Health Promotion (1986)

  • First International Conference on Health Promotion, Ottawa, Canada.
  • Definition: “Health promotion is the process of enabling people to increase control over and to improve their health.”
  • Prerequisites for health: Peace, shelter, education, food, income, stable ecosystem, sustainable resources, social justice, equity.

Five Action Areas (mnemonic: B.R.S.D.R.)

  • Build Healthy Public Policy
  • Create Supportive Environments
  • Strengthen Community Action
  • Develop Personal Skills
  • Reorient Health Services

Key principle: Health promotion goes beyond healthcare – integrates political, economic, social, and environmental strategies.

Health Belief Model (HBM) – Rosenstock & Becker

  • Explains why people take health actions based on their perceptions.
  • Key constructs:
  • Perceived susceptibility – belief about getting a disease.
  • Perceived severity – belief about seriousness of the disease.
  • Perceived benefits – belief in effectiveness of recommended action.
  • Perceived barriers – obstacles to taking action (cost, inconvenience).
  • Cues to action – triggers that prompt action (symptoms, media, advice).
  • Self‑efficacy – confidence to perform the behavior (added later).

Example: A woman getting a mammogram because she believes she is at risk (susceptibility), breast cancer is serious (severity), mammogram detects early cancer (benefit), and her doctor reminded her (cue to action).

PHN use: Assess client's beliefs and address barriers to improve compliance.

Transtheoretical Model (Stages of Change) – Prochaska & DiClemente

  • Precontemplation – not intending to change in next 6 months.
  • Contemplation – considering change within 6 months.
  • Preparation – planning to take action within 30 days.
  • Action – actively making changes (less than 6 months).
  • Maintenance – sustaining change for more than 6 months.
  • Termination – complete confidence; no relapse temptation.

Processes of change: consciousness raising, self‑reevaluation, counterconditioning, stimulus control, reinforcement management, etc.

PHN application: Tailor interventions to the client's stage (e.g., provide information for contemplation; encourage action steps for preparation).

PRECEDE-PROCEED Model – Green & Kreuter

PRECEDE (Predisposing, Reinforcing, Enabling Constructs in Educational/Environmental Diagnosis) – planning phase.

  • Phase 1: Social diagnosis
  • Phase 2: Epidemiological diagnosis
  • Phase 3: Behavioral & environmental diagnosis
  • Phase 4: Educational & ecological diagnosis (Predisposing, Reinforcing, Enabling factors)
  • Phase 5: Administrative & policy diagnosis

PROCEED (Policy, Regulatory, Organizational Constructs in Educational & Environmental Development) – implementation & evaluation.

Key constructs:

  • Predisposing factors: knowledge, attitudes, beliefs, values.
  • Enabling factors: skills, resources, accessibility.
  • Reinforcing factors: feedback, social support, incentives.

PHN use: Comprehensive community assessment and program planning.

Pender's Health Promotion Model (HPM)

  • Focuses on positive health and well‑being rather than disease prevention.
  • Emphasizes individual characteristics and experiences, behavior-specific cognitions (perceived benefits, barriers, self‑efficacy), and behavioral outcomes.
  • Components: Prior related behavior, personal factors, perceived benefits/barriers, perceived self‑efficacy, activity‑related affect, interpersonal & situational influences, commitment to action, immediate competing demands, health‑promoting behavior.
  • Widely used in nursing for lifestyle interventions (exercise, nutrition, stress management).

PHN role: Help clients set achievable health goals and enhance self‑efficacy through small successes and positive reinforcement.

Applying Health Promotion Models in PHN Practice

  • Ottawa Charter: Advocate for smoke‑free ordinances (healthy public policy), create walking paths (supportive environment), organize community fitness groups (strengthen community action), teach life skills (develop personal skills), ensure RHU offers health screening (reorient health services).
  • HBM: Identify perceived barriers to vaccination, provide cues to action (reminders, incentives).
  • TTM: Match smoking cessation intervention to client's stage (e.g., precontemplation vs. action).
  • PRECEDE-PROCEED: Conduct community diagnosis to identify predisposing, enabling, reinforcing factors before designing a nutrition program.
  • Pender's HPM: Help clients build self‑efficacy for exercise by setting small, achievable goals and celebrating progress.

Best practice: Use multiple models complementarily – e.g., PRECEDE for planning, HBM for message framing, TTM for stage‑matched interventions, Ottawa for policy advocacy.
